\begin{abstract}
\noindent A fixed pessimistic belief distortion has little price relevance for a safe sovereign or for one that already prices default. It matters near the default boundary. This paper places that observation in a sovereign pricing model with narrative diffusion and debt-service feedback. The primitive belief gap is exactly single-peaked. The equilibrium price effect is single-peaked under a derivative restriction on the feedback multiplier, and the restriction holds on the reported scenario grid. Narrative adoption follows from an attention-cost rule in which investors adopt when decision value exceeds an idiosyncratic cost. The prevalence process is continuous in time and the annual pricing model is mapped exactly into monthly simulation. A state-space measurement equation separates media coverage from latent investor prevalence. In the quantitative scenario, ten percentage points of latent prevalence raise the spread by as much as \RelPeakTen\ basis points, and seeding raises the two-year default probability by at most \WedgePeak\ percentage points. These are scenario outputs. A binding cap sets the marginal narrative effect on the modelled bond to zero. It does not eliminate the narrative or its effects on other markets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

On July 26, 2012, the president of the European Central Bank stated that the ECB was ready to do whatever was necessary within its mandate to preserve the euro. The Outright Monetary Transactions program was announced in September and was never activated. Research attributes a large fall in Italian and Spanish short yields to the OMT announcements \citep{AltavillaEtAl2016}. The intervention also changed redenomination insurance and the expected policy regime \citep{KrishnamurthyEtAl2018}. It was not a pure communication shock.

This paper asks when a portable pessimistic claim can move a sovereign spread. The answer comes from the default boundary. Narrative economics supplies the diffusion idea \citep{Shiller2017,FlynnSastry2024}. Sovereign pricing supplies the state dependence. The new object is the interaction between a default-probability curve and a measurable media-diffusion process.

The direct belief gap is $G(z)=\Phi(z+\Delta)-\Phi(z)$. It is the probability mass between the ordinary and pessimistic default thresholds. This gap has one peak at $z=-\Delta/2$ and vanishes in both tails. The equilibrium effect multiplies $G$ by the spread-feedback term. A bounded multiplier does not preserve single-peakedness by itself. Proposition~\ref{prop:eqhump} states a derivative condition that does, and the quantitative scenario verifies that condition on its state space.

Adoption has a costly-attention foundation. An investor transmits the narrative when its decision value exceeds an idiosyncratic cost. The resulting adoption probability is the cost distribution evaluated at the price relevance of the narrative. This assumption is testable against a competing rule based on perceived mispricing. Media coverage measures diffusion across outlets. It is linked to latent investor prevalence by a measurement equation rather than equated with it.

When $R_0<1$, a small seed decays. When $R_0>1$, it grows while fundamentals remain in the susceptible interval. Debt-service feedback can then change the fundamentals path. The instantaneous lower zero-drift root rises with prevalence under a local slope condition. This root is not automatically the basin boundary of the full system. The quantitative section therefore reports the vector field and the seeded path. In that scenario the seeded path crosses zero capacity in month \ZoneDefMonth, while the matched unseeded path survives.

The quantitative claims are asymmetric. Narratives in this model move prices much more than they move defaults. At the hump's peak, ten percentage points of prevalence are worth \RelPeakTen\ basis points of spread, and a full outbreak generates an excess spread that reaches \PeakExcess\ basis points before the end: magnitudes in the range of the fundamentals-unexplained spread components documented for the euro periphery by \citet{DeGrauweJi2013}. The same outbreak adds up to \WedgePeak\ percentage points to the two-year default hazard at the baseline feedback, a band of \WedgeChiLo\ to \WedgeChiHi\ across the range of gross financing needs that disciplines the feedback parameter, all inside the zone. For a safe sovereign the addition is \WedgeSafe\ percentage points. A story taxes vulnerable sovereigns heavily while it circulates, and occasionally, in a narrow band of fundamentals, finishes one off.

The policy experiments change carriers, abandonment, or the modelled spread. One-time removal delays a supercritical outbreak under the local constant-rate approximation. Faster abandonment lowers the reproduction number. A binding cap sets the marginal narrative effect on the capped bond to zero. Other market and political channels remain outside the model. The timing costs reported below are conditional on the scenario parameters.

The model combines the narrative diffusion framework of \citet{FlynnSastry2024} with the sovereign default boundary and debt-service feedback \citep{Calvo1988,LorenzoniWerning2019}. Text methods supply the observable media process \citep{GentzkowKellyTaddy2019}. The contribution is the default-boundary prediction and its separation of primitive from equilibrium state dependence.

The empirical design uses GDELT as the public media core and licensed full text for historical validation. Scheduled fiscal releases discipline timing. Boundary proximity is predetermined and estimated from the lagged response of spreads to fundamental surprises. OMT is treated as a compound event rather than an instrument for narrative removal.

\section{A Sovereign, Its Creditors, and a Story}\label{sec:model}

The threshold-default primitive delivers the direct pricing hump. Equilibrium single-peakedness requires a derivative restriction on the feedback term. Contagion results also require the costly-attention adoption assumption. Global default paths require phase conditions beyond the local comparative statics.

\subsection{Fundamentals, default, and pricing}

Time is monthly and the pricing horizon is one year. A sovereign's fundamentals $\theta_t$ measure debt-service capacity in units of annual resources. Fundamentals mean-revert and are drained by borrowing costs:
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1} = \rho_m \theta_t + (1 - \rho_m)\bar\theta - \chi_m s_t + \varepsilon_{t+1},
\qquad \varepsilon \sim N(0, \sigma_m^2),
\label{eq:theta-law}
\end{equation}
where $s_t$ is an annualized spread. Default is absorbing once $\theta_t<0$. The monthly coefficients aggregate exactly to the annual law under a constant spread:
\begin{equation}
\rho_m=\rho^{1/12},
\qquad
\sigma_m=\sigma\sqrt{\frac{1-\rho_m^2}{1-\rho^2}},
\qquad
\chi_m=\chi\frac{1-\rho_m}{1-\rho}.
\label{eq:aggregation}
\end{equation}
Thus twelve monthly innovations have variance $\sigma^2$, and twelve monthly spread charges cumulate to $\chi s$ after persistence is accounted for. At the annual horizon, the perceived default probability is
\begin{equation}
p(\theta, s) = \Phi\!\left( z(\theta, s) \right),
\qquad
z(\theta, s) = \frac{-\rho\theta - (1-\rho)\bar\theta + \chi s}{\sigma}.
\label{eq:pdef}
\end{equation}
The S-shape of $p$ in $\theta$ is the paper's load-bearing object, and it comes free: any default technology in which a shock-buffeted state crosses a threshold produces one.

A pessimistic narrative circulates among the sovereign's creditors. Carrying the story means pricing the bond under fundamentals worse by $\xi$: the deficit is understated, the peg will break. The story is portable and self-contained, which is what lets it spread. Its content is a belief distortion rather than an information signal. With prevalence $n_t$, the fraction of investor wealth carrying the story, the market-clearing default assessment is the wealth-weighted average
\begin{equation}
\bar p(\theta, s, n) = (1 - n)\, \Phi(z) + n\, \Phi(z + \Delta),
\qquad \Delta \equiv \frac{\rho \xi}{\sigma},
\label{eq:pbar}
\end{equation}
so the story enters as a shift of size $\Delta$ in the standardized distance to default for the infected. Risk-neutral pricing of one-period debt with recovery $\mathcal{R}$ gives the spread as expected loss, $s = (1 - \mathcal{R})\bar p$, and since the spread feeds back into the default assessment through \eqref{eq:pdef}, the within-period equilibrium is a fixed point.

\begin{lemma}[Pricing equilibrium and the doom-loop multiplier]\label{lem:pricing}
If $(1-\mathcal{R})\chi / (\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}) < 1$, the map $s \mapsto (1-\mathcal{R})\bar p(\theta, s, n)$ is a contraction: for every $(\theta, n)$ there is a unique equilibrium spread $s^*(\theta, n)$, increasing in $n$ and decreasing in $\theta$. Small shocks to the default assessment are amplified by the multiplier
\[
M(\theta, n) = \Big[ 1 - (1-\mathcal{R})\,\tfrac{\chi}{\sigma}\big((1-n)\phi(z^*) + n\,\phi(z^* + \Delta)\big) \Big]^{-1} \,\ge\, 1,
\]
which reaches \MultPeak\ at the scenario maximum. If the feedback crosses the contraction frontier, multiple pricing fixed points can arise.
\end{lemma}

The scenario sits inside the uniqueness region with maximal loop gain \GainMax.

\subsection{The contagion block}

Prevalence follows a continuous-time adoption process:
\begin{equation}
\dot n_t=\beta(\theta_t,n_t)n_t(1-n_t)-\gamma n_t.
\label{eq:sir}
\end{equation}
This vector field leaves $[0,1]$ invariant whenever $\beta$ and $\gamma$ are nonnegative. The monthly simulation applies the exact logistic flow over one month while holding the current rates fixed.

\begin{assumption}[Costly attention and adoption]\label{ass:adoption}
Upon encountering a carrier, investor $i$ draws an adoption cost $c_i$ from a distribution $H_c$ on the nonnegative real line. Adoption occurs when
\[
c_i\leq\lambda\left|\frac{\partial s^*(\theta,n)}{\partial n}\right|.
\]
The distribution has no mass at zero and has a strictly positive density on its interior. Encounters occur at rate $\bar m A$. Hence
\[
\beta(\theta,n)=\bar m A H_c\!\left(\lambda\left|\frac{\partial s^*(\theta,n)}{\partial n}\right|\right).
\]
\end{assumption}

The inequality gives the reduced-form adoption function an attention-cost interpretation. It does not make the distorted belief rational. A competing hypothesis makes adoption depend on perceived mispricing rather than decision value. The two mechanisms need not respond alike when a policy caps one sovereign yield. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:adoption}, the reproduction number at zero prevalence is
\begin{equation}
R_0(\theta)=\frac{\bar m A H_c\!\left(\lambda v(\theta,0)\right)}{\gamma},
\qquad v(\theta,n)=\frac{\partial s^*(\theta,n)}{\partial n}.
\label{eq:r0}
\end{equation}
The reproduction number is high where adoption value is high.

\section{The Boundary Theorems}\label{sec:boundary}

\subsection{Where stories are priced}

\begin{theorem}[Primitive pricing hump]\label{prop:hump}
Let $G(z)=\Phi(z+\Delta)-\Phi(z)$ for $\Delta>0$. The function $G$ is strictly increasing on $(-\infty,-\Delta/2)$, strictly decreasing on $(-\Delta/2,\infty)$, and converges to zero in both tails. Its unique maximum is $2\Phi(\Delta/2)-1$. Because $z^*(\theta,n)$ is strictly decreasing in $\theta$, the direct belief gap $G(z^*)$ is single-peaked in fundamentals.
\end{theorem}

The equilibrium price impact is
\[
\frac{\partial s^*}{\partial n} = (1 - \mathcal{R})\,\big[\Phi(z^* + \Delta) - \Phi(z^*)\big]\, M(\theta, n).
\]
\begin{proposition}[Sufficient condition for an equilibrium hump]\label{prop:eqhump}
Put $a=(1-\mathcal R)\chi/\sigma$ and $d(z,n)=(1-n)\phi(z)+n\phi(z+\Delta)$. Define
\[
Q(z,n)=\frac{G'(z)}{G(z)}+\frac{a d'(z,n)}{1-a d(z,n)}.
\]
On any index interval $\mathcal Z$, suppose $a d(z,n)<1$ and $\partial Q(z,n)/\partial z<0$. The equilibrium price impact has at most one interior peak on the corresponding fundamentals interval. It has exactly one peak if $Q$ takes both signs. In the quantitative scenario, the largest value of $\partial Q/\partial z$ on $z\in[-6,6]$ and $n\in[0,1]$ is \HumpMargin. The computed equilibrium effect is therefore single-peaked on the reported state space. In the omitted tails, $G$ is numerically negligible and $M$ converges to one.
\end{proposition}

The theorem concerns the direct belief gap. The proposition states the additional restriction required for equilibrium feedback to preserve one peak. Boundedness of the multiplier alone is not sufficient.

\subsection{Where stories spread}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:adoption}, adoption is an increasing function of the equilibrium price impact. The interval result therefore uses Proposition~\ref{prop:eqhump}.

\begin{proposition}[The susceptible zone]\label{prop:zone}
Suppose Proposition~\ref{prop:eqhump} holds and $H_c$ is strictly increasing over the attained values. Then $R_0(\theta)$ is single-peaked with vanishing tails. The susceptible set $Z=\{\theta:R_0(\theta)\geq1\}$ is an interval. It is nonempty if and only if $\bar m A H_c(\lambda v_{\max})\geq\gamma$. A rise in $A$ expands every superlevel set. A rise in $\gamma$ contracts it. Other parameter changes alter the height and location of $v$, so endpoint monotonicity requires additional restrictions.
\end{proposition}

In the scenario, reducing amplification to one quarter of baseline shrinks the susceptible interval to width \ZoneWidthPre\ in capacity units. At baseline the interval maps into spreads between \ZoneSpreadLo\ and \ZoneSpreadHi\ basis points. These are model coordinates, not estimated historical boundaries.

\begin{corollary}[Boundary proxy]\label{cor:stat}
For small $\Delta$, the direct belief gap satisfies $G(z)=\Delta\phi(z)+O(\Delta^2)$. The lagged local sensitivity of spreads to scheduled fundamental surprises is therefore a model-based proxy for boundary proximity. It is not a sufficient statistic once the multiplier, narrative bite, or recovery varies across countries.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{From local drift to global paths}

The state $(\theta_t,n_t)$ moves through a plane. At zero prevalence, a small seed initially decays where $R_0<1$ and grows where $R_0>1$. When $\beta$ is constant, the positive steady state is $1-\gamma/\beta$. In the model $\beta$ varies with both state variables, so global behavior must be read from the nullclines and vector field.

\begin{proposition}[Instantaneous drift boundary]\label{prop:cliff}
Let
\[
D(\theta,n)=(1-\rho_m)(\bar\theta-\theta)-\chi_m s^*(\theta,n).
\]
Suppose that for each $n$ in an interval $N$, the equation $D(\theta,n)=0$ has a lower root $\hat\theta(n)>0$ with $D_\theta(\hat\theta(n),n)>0$. Then $\hat\theta$ is continuously differentiable and
\[
\hat\theta'(n)=\frac{\chi_m s_n^*(\hat\theta(n),n)}{D_\theta(\hat\theta(n),n)}>0.
\]
This root is the instantaneous lower drift boundary for the scalar fundamentals equation when $n$ is held fixed. It is not by itself the basin boundary of the two-dimensional system.
\end{proposition}

Different seeded and unseeded outcomes require global phase restrictions. A forward-invariant default region must have $D\leq-d<0$. The seeded path must enter that region while the unseeded path remains in a safe invariant set. Under these conditions the seeded path reaches default in finite time and the unseeded path does not. Figure~\ref{fig:phase} reports the nullclines and vector field for the scenario. Figure~\ref{fig:outbreak} reports the simulated path. The seeded path crosses zero capacity in month \ZoneDefMonth. This is a scenario path result, not a consequence of root monotonicity alone.

\section{Policy Experiments}\label{sec:policy}

\begin{proposition}[Policy effects within the model]\label{prop:policy}
If $R_0>1$ is constant while prevalence remains near zero, removal of a fraction $f$ delays return to the original prevalence by
\[
\frac{\log[1/(1-f)]}{\gamma(R_0-1)}.
\]
A rise in $\gamma$ lowers $R_0$ pointwise and lowers the endemic prevalence wherever $\beta$ is constant. A perfectly credible cap on the modelled bond sets $\partial s^*/\partial n=0$ wherever it binds. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:adoption}, adoption through the modelled price-relevance channel then stops in that region. The cap also truncates the feedback from that bond spread to fundamentals.
\end{proposition}

The cap result concerns one pricing channel. Narratives may still affect CDS, other maturities, auction quantities, capital flight, bank funding, or political choices. A policy backstop can also change expected recovery and the policy regime. OMT is therefore a compound intervention rather than a pure narrative experiment. The calculations below impose a cap on the modelled spread and hold other channels fixed.

If adoption tracks decision value, a binding cap lowers media diffusion through this channel. If adoption tracks perceived mispricing, coverage can persist while the capped yield is stable. Post-OMT text can describe which pattern occurred. It cannot isolate this mechanism because OMT changed tail insurance, conditionality, and the expected policy regime.

\section{Measurement and Empirical Designs}\label{sec:evidence}

\subsection{Text and the latent narrative state}

The public core corpus is GDELT 2.0 Global Knowledge Graph and Mentions data, which are downloadable and available through BigQuery \citep{GDELTData}. Factiva or LexisNexis provides licensed full-text validation for historical episodes. The design does not require private messages, investor chat logs, restricted social-platform archives, or wealth-weighted communication networks.

A narrative is a structured claim. It names an economic state, gives a causal explanation, implies a future sovereign outcome, and carries an action or policy implication. A high-recall dictionary screen selects candidates. A multilingual classifier assigns the structured label. The validation sample is manually double-coded under blinded assignment. The prompt, model version, label rules, and country-language error matrices are frozen before outcome analysis.

Text coverage is not investor wealth prevalence. The measurement equation is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{TextIndex}_{it}=a_i+\lambda_i n_{it}+e_{it},
\qquad
n_{i,t+1}=\mathcal T(n_{it},\theta_{it})+\eta_{it}.
\label{eq:measurement7}
\end{equation}
A state-space implementation estimates the latent $n_{it}$ and the loading $\lambda_i$. A reduced-form implementation uses the text index as a proxy and does not convert coverage changes into percentage points of investor wealth. GDELT timestamps and source identifiers measure media diffusion across outlets. They do not measure person-to-person investor transmission.

\subsection{Boundary interaction and release timing}

The workhorse local projection is
\begin{equation}
\Delta s_{i,t+h}=\alpha_i+\tau_t+f_h(B_{i,t-1})N_{it}+\Gamma_h'X_{i,t-1}+u_{i,t+h},
\label{eq:lp7}
\end{equation}
where $N_{it}$ is the measured text index or the filtered latent state. The function $f_h$ is estimated by bins or splines. The theory predicts a small effect in both tails and an interior peak. A linear interaction is not the primary specification.

The boundary proxy $B_{i,t-1}$ is estimated before the narrative window. It is the lagged local sensitivity of spreads to scheduled fundamental surprises. An alternative is constructed from debt service, reserves, maturity structure, and primary-balance forecasts. Contemporaneous spreads are excluded from the proxy because they would create a mechanical narrative-by-spread interaction.

For scheduled fiscal release $e$, estimate
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Takeup}_{ie}&=a_i+\tau_e+\pi_1\mathrm{Surprise}_{ie}
+\pi_2\mathrm{Surprise}_{ie}B_{i,e-1}+\varepsilon_{ie},
\label{eq:takeup7}\\
\Delta s_{ie}&=b_1\mathrm{Surprise}_{ie}+b_2\widehat\varepsilon_{ie}
+b_3\widehat\varepsilon_{ie}B_{i,e-1}+u_{ie}.
\label{eq:release7}
\end{align}
Eurostat publishes dated calendars for government deficit and debt releases \citep{EurostatCalendar}. Residual take-up remains endogenous to unobserved news. The release design improves timing and conditioning. It does not create random narrative exposure.

\subsection{Supportive variation and interpretation}

Foreign media diffusion can be summarized by $N_{it}^{F}=\sum_{j\neq i}w_{ij,0}N_{jt}$ using predetermined language and media-overlap weights. Common investors, bank exposures, trade, and regional news threaten the exclusion restriction. This design is supportive unless those channels are directly absorbed and the remaining overlap shifts coverage without shifting repayment capacity.

The OMT episode is an interrupted event study. It changed tail-risk insurance, conditionality, and the expected policy regime. It is useful for comparing narrative volume, diffusion speed, sovereign yields, and CDS around a major backstop. It is not a pure test of narrative deletion. Lead-lag local projections can test whether text tends to precede prices near the estimated boundary. Granger timing is a dynamic fingerprint, not causal identification.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

The default boundary gives a precise restriction on narrative pricing. A fixed pessimistic shift has a direct price effect equal to the normal probability mass between two default thresholds. That effect has one peak. Equilibrium feedback preserves one peak only under an additional derivative condition. The condition is stated analytically and verified on the quantitative state space.

Narrative adoption is tied to decision value through an attention-cost distribution. The prevalence equation is continuous in time. Media diffusion remains an observed proxy linked to latent investor prevalence through a measurement equation. These distinctions keep a text count from being interpreted as a wealth share without evidence.

The numerical results are scenarios. At the stated inputs, ten percentage points of latent prevalence can raise spreads by \RelPeakTen\ basis points near the boundary, and a seed can raise the two-year default probability by \WedgePeak\ percentage points. The phase portrait is needed to establish whether a local drift movement becomes a global default path. A spread cap removes the modeled marginal effect only where it binds. OMT changed several policy objects at once and cannot identify narrative deletion by itself.

\section{Proofs}\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Lemma \ref{lem:pricing}}
Write $F(s)=(1-\mathcal R)\bar p(\theta,s,n)$. Then
\[
F'(s)=(1-\mathcal R)\frac{\chi}{\sigma}
\big[(1-n)\phi(z)+n\phi(z+\Delta)\big]
\leq\frac{(1-\mathcal R)\chi}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}<1.
\]
The map sends $[0,1-\mathcal R]$ into itself and is a contraction. Monotonicity follows by implicit differentiation because the denominator $1-F'(s^*)$ is positive. The same differentiation gives the multiplier. A cap replaces $F$ by $\min\{F,\bar s\}$, which is Lipschitz with the same contraction modulus. Failure of the sufficient bound does not prove multiplicity. It permits it when the fixed-point map crosses the 45-degree line at the required tangencies. $\blacksquare$

\subsection{Proof of Theorem \ref{prop:hump}}
Let $G(z)=\Phi(z+\Delta)-\Phi(z)=\int_z^{z+\Delta}\phi(u)\,du$. Since $\phi'(u)=-u\phi(u)$,
\[
G'(z)=\phi(z+\Delta)-\phi(z).
\]
For $\Delta>0$, equality $\phi(z+\Delta)=\phi(z)$ holds iff $(z+\Delta)^2=z^2$, hence iff $z=-\Delta/2$. If $z<-\Delta/2$, then $|z+\Delta|<|z|$ and $G'(z)>0$. If $z>-\Delta/2$, then $|z+\Delta|>|z|$ and $G'(z)<0$. Thus $G$ is single-peaked at $-\Delta/2$ and converges to zero in both tails. Differentiating the pricing fixed point gives
\[
\frac{dz^*}{d\theta}=-\frac{\rho}{\sigma}\,M(\theta,n)<0,
\]
so the single-peaked profile in $z$ maps into a single-peaked profile in fundamentals. At $z=-\Delta/2$, symmetry gives $G=2\Phi(\Delta/2)-1$. $\blacksquare$

\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:eqhump}}
For fixed $n$, write the equilibrium price derivative as
\[
v(z,n)=(1-\mathcal R)\frac{G(z)}{1-a d(z,n)}.
\]
The contraction condition makes the denominator positive. Logarithmic differentiation gives
\[
\frac{\partial\log v(z,n)}{\partial z}=Q(z,n).
\]
If $Q_z<0$, the log derivative is strictly decreasing and has at most one zero. If it takes a positive and a negative value, that zero exists and is the unique maximum. Since $z^*(\theta,n)$ is strictly decreasing in $\theta$, the same uniqueness holds in fundamentals. The reported numerical margin evaluates the analytic derivative
\[
Q_z=(\log G)''+
\frac{a\{d''(1-ad)+a(d')^2\}}{(1-ad)^2}
\]
on the stated grid. $\blacksquare$

\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:zone}}
Proposition~\ref{prop:eqhump} makes $v(\theta,0)$ single-peaked with zero tails. Strict monotonicity of $H_c$ preserves that ordering. Multiplication by $\bar mA/\gamma$ preserves it again. A continuous single-peaked function has interval superlevel sets. The set at level one is nonempty exactly when its maximum weakly exceeds one. Increasing $A$ raises the function pointwise and increasing $\gamma$ lowers it pointwise, which gives the set inclusions. $\blacksquare$

\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:cliff}}
The implicit function theorem applies at the lower root because $D_\theta>0$. Lemma~\ref{lem:pricing} gives $s_n^*>0$. Hence $D_n=-\chi_m s_n^*<0$ and
\[
\hat\theta'(n)=-\frac{D_n}{D_\theta}
=\frac{\chi_m s_n^*}{D_\theta}>0.
\]
No statement about the global basin follows without restrictions on the second component of the vector field. $\blacksquare$

\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:policy}}
Near zero prevalence with constant rates, $n(t)=n(0)\exp[(\beta-\gamma)t]+o(n(0))$. Since $\beta-\gamma=\gamma(R_0-1)$, a cut from $n$ to $(1-f)n$ is undone after the stated delay. Raising $\gamma$ lowers $R_0=\beta/\gamma$ and lowers $1-\gamma/\beta$ where $\beta>\gamma$ is fixed. Where a cap binds, the modelled spread is locally constant in $n$. Assumption~\ref{ass:adoption} then gives zero adoption through decision value. The remaining law is $\dot n=-\gamma n$. The fundamentals drift also replaces the uncapped spread with the cap. $\blacksquare$

\section{The Pricing Block: Details and the Multiplicity Frontier}\label{app:pricing}

\paragraph{Spread mapping.} The baseline uses linear expected-loss pricing, $s=(1-\mathcal R)\bar p$. The alternative mapping $s=-\log[1-\bar p(1-\mathcal R)]$ is more convex at high default probability. It changes both the multiplier and the equilibrium-hump condition. No robustness claim for that mapping is used in the propositions.

\paragraph{The Calvo frontier.} The condition $(1-\mathcal R)\chi\phi(0)/\sigma<1$ is sufficient for contraction. The baseline scenario has maximal gain \GainMax. At $\chi=\ChiGFNHi$ it reaches \GainChiHi. Beyond the contraction region, uniqueness is no longer guaranteed and equilibrium selection must be specified \citep{Calvo1988,AguiarEtAl2022}.

\paragraph{Wealth-weighted pricing.} Averaging beliefs by wealth presumes infected and susceptible investors hold with equal intensity. Letting the infected also shorten duration or exit produces an effective prevalence exceeding $n$ in the pricing equation and strengthens every result. The text uses the lower-intensity baseline.

\section{Simulation Details}\label{app:sim}

\section{Data and Measurement Protocol}\label{app:data}

\paragraph{Sources.} GDELT 2.0 supplies the public media-diffusion core \citep{GDELTData}. Factiva or LexisNexis supplies licensed historical full text. Consensus forecasts measure scheduled-release surprises. Eurostat supplies dated deficit and debt releases \citep{EurostatCalendar}. World Bank QPSD and debt-office cash-flow schedules discipline the feedback block \citep{WorldBankQPSD7}.

\paragraph{Classification.} A dictionary layer supplies high recall. A frozen multilingual classifier requires the structured claim defined in Section~\ref{sec:evidence}. A blinded, double-coded sample supplies inter-rater reliability and out-of-sample error rates by country and language.

\paragraph{Construction.} The text index is the share of country-specific sovereign coverage carrying a classified narrative. Media diffusion uses first-appearance timestamps and new-outlet adoption within story clusters. Equation~\eqref{eq:measurement7} separates this observed index from latent investor prevalence. Survey beliefs can validate the loading where comparable questions exist \citep{AndreEtAl2026}.

\section{Extensions}\label{app:ext}

\paragraph{Cross-border story networks.} Let prevalence in country $i$ respond to a reach-weighted sum of foreign prevalences. This structure can generate correlated spread movements among susceptible countries and provides the model counterpart to the supportive foreign-diffusion design \citep{LongstaffEtAl2011}.

\paragraph{Endogenous amplification.} Attention to sovereign stories rises with volatility, making $A$ procyclical: the zone widens exactly when fundamentals deteriorate, an accelerant with the same signature as a rising multiplier and a natural explanation for why crisis eras feel narrative-dense.

\paragraph{Immunity and waves.} Investors who abandoned a story once may resist it: an SIRS structure with slow waning of immunity produces refractory periods after outbreaks and recurring waves at business-cycle frequency, testable in long text archives: the same story striking the same country twice should spread slower the second time, controlling for the boundary.

\paragraph{Heterogeneous stories.} A mixture over bite sizes $\xi$ concentrates the priced composition toward severe stories as the boundary nears, since the hump's window widens with $\Delta$: near the boundary, discourse should grow and darken, a compositional prediction the classification layer can test.
